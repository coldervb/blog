## The core question

Central banks spend enormous resources measuring inflation. But this paper asks a different question: does it matter what *people think* inflation will be, even if their expectations are wrong?

The short answer is yes, enormously. And households form those expectations in surprisingly mundane ways.

## What the paper found

D'Acunto, Malmendier, and Weber ran a large-scale survey across the US and Germany, tracking how households form inflation expectations over time. Their key finding:

**People anchor inflation expectations to the prices they personally encounter most often, especially groceries.**

This seems obvious, but it has non-obvious consequences. Grocery prices are volatile and highly visible. A spike in egg or bread prices can cause a household to expect 8% inflation economy-wide, even when the actual rate is 3%. Meanwhile, large price changes in infrequent purchases (cars, appliances) have almost no effect on expectations.

## Why this matters for policy

Monetary policy works partly through expectations management. If the Fed raises rates to curb inflation, part of the intended effect is that households and businesses *believe* inflation will fall and adjust behaviour accordingly. If people stop spending because they expect prices to rise further, that itself reduces demand and inflation.

But if expectations are anchored to grocery prices rather than Fed communications, central bank credibility becomes less powerful. The paper shows that:

- **Women** tend to form higher inflation expectations than men, because they do more grocery shopping and encounter volatile food prices more frequently
- **Lower-income households** show larger expectation errors because they shop at stores with higher price volatility
- **Exposure to past hyperinflation** (in Germany's case) makes households systematically more pessimistic about future inflation

## The mechanism

The paper's theoretical model is straightforward: people use readily available, frequently observed price signals as proxies for general inflation. It is a rational heuristic given that most people do not read CPI reports.

The problem is that the proxy is biased. Food and energy prices are excluded from *core* inflation precisely because they are so volatile. But they are exactly what people observe daily.

## What this changes

Before this paper, most monetary models assumed households update inflation expectations based on central bank announcements and economic data. This paper suggests the actual input is much more granular and much harder to control.

Policy implications are uncomfortable:

1. **Communication strategies aimed at educated consumers** (press releases, speeches) may have limited reach among the households whose expectations matter most for demand
2. **Grocery price stability** may be a more effective tool for anchoring expectations than economists traditionally thought
3. **Demographic heterogeneity in expectations** means aggregate inflation expectations mask wide variation, so a single policy response fits poorly

## The bottom line

Inflation expectations are not formed rationally from aggregate data. They are formed experientially, from the prices people see every week at the supermarket. That makes managing expectations far harder, and far more dependent on the mundane price stability of everyday goods, than most macro models assume.

If central banks want to shape expectations, they may need to think less about press conferences and more about the price of milk.
