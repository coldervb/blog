There is a common assumption in economics that poor people make bad decisions because they lack discipline, education, or motivation. This paper argues something far more uncomfortable: they might just be too stressed to think straight.

Researchers ran a field experiment with 408 low-income workers in rural Odisha, India, during the agricultural lean season, when there is barely any farming work and people take whatever short-term jobs they can find. The job was simple: stitch leaves together to make disposable plates for restaurants. Workers were paid per plate, so the more they made, the more they earned.

The twist? Some workers got paid early.

Same job, same wage, same hours. The only difference was that the treatment group received an interim payment partway through the two-week contract, while the control group waited until the end. The researchers then tracked whether this changed how productive workers were.

It did. By a lot.

## What Actually Happened

The day after receiving the cash, treated workers increased their output by around 7%. They also made fewer mistakes: fewer botched stitches, fewer misplaced leaves, fewer errors they had to undo. They were working faster and more carefully at the same time.

What is interesting is that this did not happen when workers were simply told they would be paid early. It only kicked in once the money was actually in their hands. Announcing the payment in advance did nothing to productivity. Receiving it did everything.

The researchers also tracked what workers did with the money immediately. On the same evening they got paid, treated workers repaid loans at a rate 746% higher than the control group. They also spent more on food, soap, fuel, and clothing. The cash went directly to the things that had been stressing them out.

## Why This Matters

The researchers framed it as a bandwidth problem. Financial stress does not just feel bad, it occupies mental space that would otherwise be used for focus, planning, and attention. When you are constantly running through how you will repay a moneylender, or what happens if your child gets sick and you have nothing saved, that is cognitive load you carry into every hour of your workday.

The paper measured this directly. They looked at physical markers on each plate: the number of double holes where a stitch was removed to fix a mistake, extra leaves, and unnecessary stitches. These all went down for workers who received the cash infusion. They were not just working faster. They were thinking more clearly.

The researchers also cross-checked this against what happens when you simply raise the piece rate. Higher pay did increase output, but it had zero effect on the attentiveness measures. Workers hurried more but did not plan better. The psychological benefit of cash relief and the motivational benefit of higher wages are genuinely different things.

## The Uncomfortable Implication

The productivity effects were concentrated among the poorest workers in the sample. Not a surprise since they had more financial strain to begin with, so relieving it had more room to help.

But consider what this means. The people who most need to earn money are the ones least able to focus on earning it, precisely because they do not have enough. It is not a moral failure. It is a structural trap that plays out quietly in every hour of every workday.

The authors are careful not to overclaim. They are not saying pay people more and poverty disappears. But they are saying the psychological cost of financial stress is real, measurable, and directly affects economic outcomes in ways standard models do not account for. That is worth paying attention to.
